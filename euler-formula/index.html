Euler's Formula

Previously, we saw that `e^x`, `sin(θ)` and `cos(θ)` can all be written as Maclaurin series, and that these series are very similar.

`e^x` `=` `1` `+` `x` `+` `x^2/(2!)` `+` `x^3/(3!)` `+` `x^4/(4!)` `+` `x^5/(5!)` `+` `x^6/(6!)` `+` `x^7/(7!)`
`sin(θ) + cos(θ)` `=` `1` `+` `θ` `-` `θ^2/(2!)` `-` `θ^3/(3!)` `+` `θ^4/(4!)` `+` `x^5/(5!)` `-` `θ^6/(6!)` `-` `x^7/(7!)`

Taking what we know about the cycle of values created when we raise `i` to increasing powers, we can now write the Maclaurin series for `e^(ix)`.

So, wherever an `x` appears in the expansion of `e^x`, we will now write `ix`.

`e^x = 1 + x + (x^2)/(2!) + (x^3)/(3!) + (x^4)/(4!) + (x^5)/(5!) + ...`

becomes

`e^(ix) = 1 + ix + (ix)^2/(2!) + (ix)^3/(3!) + (ix)^4/(4!) + (ix)^5/(5!) + ...`

now reduce the various powers of `i` down to their simplest form

`e^(ix) = 1 + ix - x^2/(2!) - (ix^3)/(3!) + x^4/(4!) + (ix^5)/(5!) + ...`

We can now see that all the terms raised to an even power have no `i` in them, and all the terms raised to an odd power do have an `i`. So we can rearrange the terms to group all the odd powers together, then factor out the `i`

`e^(ix) = (1 - x^2/(2!) + x^4/(4!) - …) + i(x - (x^3)/(3!) + (x^5)/(5!) - …)`

This might start to look familiar. All the even powered terms are the Maclaurin series for `cos(θ)` and all the odd powered terms are the Maclaurin series for `sin(θ)`

So we can now take the last step and replace the groups of terms for their `sin` and `cos` equivalents:

`e^(ix) = cos(x) + i sin(x)`

This is known as Euler's Formula

Euler's Identity - A Special Case of Euler's Formula

In the lesson about measuring angles in radians, we saw that if you create an arc around the circuference of a circle whose length equals exactly one radius, then the angle you have created measures 1 radian. The key point about radians is that we are now measuring rotation in multiples of `pi` — there are `2 pi` radians in a full circle.

Let's see what happens to Euler's formula if we set `x = pi` radians (that is, we're measuring a half rotation).

`e^(i pi) = cos(pi) + i sin(pi)`

Firstly, `cos(pi) = -1` and secondly, `sin(pi) = 0`, so we get

`e^(i pi) = -1 + i xx 0`
`e^(i pi) = -1`

Now add `1` to both sides, and we get the famous Euler identity

`e^(i pi) + 1 = 0`

This formula has been described as one of the most beautiful in all of mathematics because it shows how the 5 most fundamental constants are related:

  • `e` - The natural base
  • `i` - The square root of `-1`
  • `pi` - The ratio of the diameter of a circle to its circumference
  • `1` - The fundamental counting unit
  • `0` - The midpoint of the real number line
