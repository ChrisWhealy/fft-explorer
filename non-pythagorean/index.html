The Non-Pythagorean Theorem

"The area of the square on the hypotenuse is equal to
the sum of the areas of the squares on the other two sides"

Although no writings of Pythagoras have ever been discovered, by the Late Roman Period, he was already held in high regard as a philosopher and mathematician. Consequently, the discovery of this relationship had already been attributed to him in spite of the fact that it had been widely known for at least 1000 years before his birth.

One of the earliest documents known to record this relationship is the Berlin Papyrus 6619 written in Ancient Egypt during the 13th Dynasty (1990 - 1800 BC).

All that to say, as you drag point `C` around the circle, you'll see that the sum of the areas of the two green squares always equals the area of the blue square.

The Origin of a Trigonometric Identity

Since we have decided to assign this circle a radius of one unit, the definitions of sin(θ) and cos(θ) can be reduced simply to sin(θ) = height and cos(θ) = width.

This also means that the areas of the various squares shown above will be:

  • The area of the square on the radius (the hypotenuse) is always equal to 1
  • The area of the square opposite the angle is always equal to height2 which is the same as sin(θ)2
  • The area of the square adjacent to the angle is always equal to width2 which is the same as cos(θ)2
We now have a simple, visual demonstration of the trigonometric identity sin(θ)2 + cos(θ)2 = 1.
