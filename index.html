A Gentle Introduction to Understanding The Fourier Transform

Understanding the Tools

  1. Where Do Sine and Cosine Waves Come From?

    You were probably taught in school that the trigonometric functions such as sine, cosine and tangent describe the relationship between the angles and the lengths of the sides in a right-angled triangle. Whilst this explanation is perfectly correct, it doesn't quite get down to the heart of the matter.

  2. Phase and Phase Shift

    In the previous example, you probably noticed that a sine wave and a cosine wave have exactly the same shape.
    The only difference is that sin(0°) = 0, but cos(0°) = 1.
    This difference is known as a phase shift.

  3. Amplitude

    We have already spoken about two of a wave's three fundamental properties: frequency and phase. Now we'll look at the third fundamental property, amplitude.

  4. The Non-Pythagorean Theorem

    "The area of the square on the hypotenuse is equal to
    the sum of the areas of the squares on the other two sides"

    This statement has been drummed into students for the last two millenia, and they have always been taught that it was the Greek mathematician and philosopher Pythagoras who first came up with this theorem.

    The theorem is perfectly correct, but it was discovered at least 1000 years before Pythagoras was born…

  5. Radians: Another Way To Measure Angles

    We are used to measuring angles in degrees, but in physics and engineering, another unit of rotation is used called the radian.

  6. Euler's Number

    In addition to `pi`, there's another mathematical constant that keeps popping up (sometimes when you least expect it) called `e`.

    In keeping with `pi`, it is impossible to calculate the exact value of `e`; but in spite of this limitation of precision, we need to understand how such a number is derived, because this will open the door to understanding other values we have already encountered.

  7. Maclaurin Series

    The infinite series used to calculate `e^x` is an example of a Maclaurin series. Many other values can be calculated using a Maclaurin series, but the two that are of particular interest to us are the series for calculating sine and cosine.

  8. Imaginary Numbers

    When you multiply a number by itself, you are "squaring" that number. Reversing this process is called "taking the square root". The point here is that when you square any of the normal counting numbers, the answer will always be positive:

    But what happens if you need to find the square root of a negative number? Its seems that this simple question causes mathematics to break.

  9. Euler's Formula and Identity

    We will now combine what we've learnt about using Maclaurin series to express the values of `e^x`, `sin(θ)` and `cos(θ)`, with what we've learnt about the cycle of values created we raising `i` to various integer powers to derive firstly, Euler's Formula, and secondly, Euler's famous Identity.

  10. Complex Numbers

    Now that we have encountered this strange-but-very-important-number-that-you-cannot-count-to called `i` or `sqrt (-1)`, we need to look at performing arithmetic using `i`.
