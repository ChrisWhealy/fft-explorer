Phase and Phase Shift

Phase

The sine and cosine waves are an examples of "periodic functions".

To say that something is "periodic" means that it does the same thing over and over again. For example, the hands on a clock are periodic — they just go round and round pointing at the same 12 hours over and over again.

To say that a mathematical function is periodic means that no matter what sequence of numbers you put into it, it will give you back a corresponding set of numbers that form a never ending cycle.

In the first lesson Where do Sine and Cosine Waves Come From?, we saw how a wave can be generated by tracing how the distance of a point on the circumference of a circle changes in relation to the X and Y axes, as that point spins around forever.

How far up or down a wave is in this cycle is known as the wave's phase

Phase Shift as Degrees

If we take the horizontal and vertical graphs from the first lesson and lay them on top of each other, we'll get the graph shown below, where the green wave is the sine and the blue wave is the cosine.

Notice that the sine wave first crosses the X axis at 0°, but the cosine wave crosses the X axis at 90°. So we can say that the cosine wave is offset from the sine wave by 90°.

This offset is known as a "phase shift".

Below, we can see two sine waves that are offset from each other by 90°.

But just a minute…  The blue "sine" wave looks exactly like the cosine wave we saw on the previous graph…

If you move the "Phase shift" slider over to the far left, then you can see that the blue wave now lines up exactly with the green wave. This is because compared to a sine wave, a cosine wave always has a phase shift of 90°.

If we now think of the blue wave above as a cosine wave instead of a sine wave, then we can see that:

`sin(θ + 90) = cos(θ)`
or
`cos(θ - 90) = sin(θ)`

Phase Shift as Time

Another way to think of phase shift is in terms of time — one wave is offset from another by a certain number of milleseconds. However, in order to express phase shift in terms of time, we need to know how rapidly that wave is going up and down.

In the first lesson Where do Sine and Cosine Waves Come From? we saw a point rotating around the circumference of a circle in an anti-clockwise direction. Now need to know how fast that point is going around.

This speed of oscillation is known as the wave's "frequency" and it has the unit `Hz` named after the German physicist Heinrich Hertz.

Let's say that this point is spinning around the circle 100 times per second: that is, one rotation every 10 milleseconds. So on the graph above, the horzontal axis displays degrees of rotation (from 0° to 360°), but we now know that scale can also represent time — in our example, 0° to 360° equals 10 milleseconds.

If we know for a wave of a particular frequency `f`, that it has rotated through an angle θ, then we can calculate how much time that took using the formula:
`t=θ/(f xx 360°)`

Similarly, we could rearrange this formula to calculate the angle `θ` given the frequency `f` and the time `t`:
`θ=t xx f xx 360°`

The Non-Pythagorean Theorem

"The square on the hypotenuse has an area equal
to the sum of the squares on the other two sides"

Although no writings of Pythagoras have ever been discovered, by the Late Roman Period, he was already held in high regard as a philosopher and mathematician. Consequently, the discovery of this relationship had already been attributed to him in spite of the fact that it had been widely known for at least 1000 years before Pythagoras' birth. One of the earliest documents known to record this relationship is the Berlin Papyrus 6619 written in Ancient Egypt during the 13th Dynasty (1990 - 1800 BC).

All that to say, as you drag point `C` around the circle, you'll see that the sum of areas of the two green squares always equals the area of the blue square.

The Origin of a Trigonometric Identity

Since we have decided to assign this circle a radius of one unit, the definitions of sin(θ) and cos(θ) can be reduced simply to sin(θ) = height and cos(θ) = width.

This also means that the areas of the various squares shown above will be:

  • The area of the square on the radius (the hypotenuse) is always equal to 1
  • The area of the square opposite the angle is always equal to height2 which is the same as sin(θ)2
  • The area of the square adjacent to the angle is always equal to width2 which is the same as cos(θ)2
We now have a simple, visual demonstration of the trigonometric identity sin(θ)2 + cos(θ)2 = 1.
