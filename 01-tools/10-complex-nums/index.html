Complex Numbers

When have seen that when you raise `i` to successive integer powers, we get a cycle of values `1 -> i -> -1 -> -i -> 1 -> i …`

When plotted on a graph, these values rotate in an anti-clockwise direction. However, the graph being used here represents a plane where the coordinates for every point are composed from both a real number (plotted horizontally on the X axis) and an imaginary number (plotted vertically on the Y axis).

This is called the "Complex Plane", and any point on the complex plane is identified by a Complex Number

The simplest way to represent the above set of rotating values is to use (X,Y) coordinates like this:

`i^0 =` `(1,0)` or `1 + 0i`
`i^1 =` `(0,1)` or `0 + i`
`i^2 =` `(-1,0)` or `-1 + 0i`
`i^3 =` `(0,-1)` or `0 - i`
`i^4 =` `(1,0)` or `1 + 0i`

Different Ways to Represent a Complex Number

Cartesian (or Rectangular) Form

When a complex number is expressed as a pair of values such as `4 + 3i`, we are identifying the point on the complex plane in terms of an (X,Y) pair of coordinates. In other words `4 + 3i` tells you to "Start at the origin facing east, then take four steps forward, turn 90° to the left, then take three more steps".

This representation is known as the Cartesian or Rectangular Form because the point is described in terms of the lengths of two sides of a right triangle.

Polar Form

But can't we just walk in a straight line from the origin directly to the point?

We certainly can, but first we need to know both how far we should walk, and second we need to know in which direction. (The convention here is that 0° is the direction of the positive X axis, and positive rotation is anti-clockwise).

Starting with the complex number `4 + 3i`, we know this describes a right-angled triangle with a base of length `4` and a height of length `3`. So using the theorem that Pythagoras did not discover, we know that the length of the hypotenuse will be the square root of the sum of the other two lengths squared.

 

`h = sqrt (4^2 + 3^2)`
`h = sqrt (25)`
`h = 5`

So, starting from the origin, we know we have to walk for a distance of `5` steps. We should also bear in mind that all the relationships we're using here are derived from the angles formed inside a circle.

That means that the `5` steps we will be taking are along the radius of a circle — but in which direction?

The direction in which we need to walk can be calculated by remembering the acronym SOHCAHTOA.

 

The last three letters "TOA" remind us that `tan(θ) = (opposite) / (adjacent)`

`tan(θ) = 3/4`
`θ = arctan(3/4)`
`θ = 36.87°`

So the polar coordinates for the point `4 + 3i` are `(5, 36.87°)`

Exponential Form

Lastly, we have the exponential form that can be derived directly from Euler's Formula.

`e^(iθ) = cos(θ) + i sin(θ)`

Remembering what we said earlier about circles, we should remember that the expression of Euler's Formula shown above is for a circle with a radius of `1`. To extend this formula to handle a circle of any radius `r`, we must include a term for the radius:

`r e^(iθ) = r(cos(θ) + i sin(θ))`

In our example, `θ = 36.87°` and `r = 5`, so `4 + 3i` expressed in exponential form is `5e^(36.87°i)`

Lastly, we should also note that the angle has been expressed in degrees rather than the more usual radians.

To convert degrees to radians, we must multiply the angle in degrees by `pi` then divide by `180`.

`(36.87° xx pi)/ 180 = 0.644`

So using radians, `4 + 3i` expressed in exponential form is `5e^(0.644i)`
