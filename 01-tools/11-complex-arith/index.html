Arithmetic with Complex Numbers

As we have just stated, our familiar arithmetic operations such as addition or division apply only to real numbers; so when we try to combine real numbers with imaginary numbers, the familiar rules of arithmetic simply break.

For example, in `3 + sqrt (-1)`, how much bigger does `3` become after adding `sqrt (-1)`?

This is a question we simply cannot answer because real and imaginary numbers don't play nicely together.

The only operation that can begin to handle `sqrt (-1)` is multiplication, but even then, we can only fully calculate values that multiply an even number of `i`'s together.

In spite of these limitations, we can still perform arithmetic operations on complex numbers.


Addition

Adding two complex numbers such as `(4 + 3i)` and `(1 - 2i)` is simply a matter of adding the real parts together, then adding the imaginary parts.

`(4 + 3i) + (1 - 2i)`
`=>` `(4 + 1) + (3i + -2i)`
`=>` `5 + i`

Subtraction

Subtracting two complex numbers such as `(4 + 3i)` and `(1 - 2i)` is simply a matter of subtracting the second real part from the first real part, then subtracting the second imaginary part from the first imaginary part.

`(4 + 3i) - (1 - 2i)`
`=>` `(4 - 1) + (3i - -2i)`
`=>` `3 + 5i`

Multiplication

Multiplying two complex numbers such `(4 + 3i)` and `(1 - 2i)` is a little more involved. There are several ways for performing multiplication, and here we will show two different approaches: the FOIL method and the area method. Hopefully, one will make intuitive sense to you.

The FOIL Method

FOIL is an acronym for First, Outside, Inside, Last that helps you remember the order in which terms are to be multiplied.

`(3 + 2i) * (1 - 2i)`
`=>` `(3 ** 1) + (3 ** -2i) + (2i ** 1) + (2i ** -2i)`
`=>` `3 - 6i + 2i - 4i^2`
`=>` `3 - 6i + 2i + 4`
`=>` `7 - 4i`

The Area Method

Another way of thinking of complex numbers is to represent the real and complex parts on the sides of a rectangle.

`(3 + 2i) ** (1 - 2i)`
`=>` Top row `(3 ** 1) + (1 ** 2i)` `+`
Bottom row `(3 ** -2i) + (2i ** -2i)`
`=>` `3 + 2i - 6i - 4i^2`
`=>` `3 - 4i + 4`
`=>` `7 - 4i`

Division

The basic problem with complex division is that our rules of arithmetic are unable to tell us how many times the imaginary number `sqrt (-1)` divides into a real number. So if we encounter a term such as `3/(2i)`, then we're completely stuck.

Like Descartes all those years ago, we can't seem to make that pesky `sqrt (-1)` go away.

Or can we?

The trick here is to perform the division in such a way that we are left only with a real value in the denominator.

But if we have a division such as `(4 + 3i)/(2-i)`, how do we go about getting rid of the `-i` in `(2-i)`?


The Complex Conjugate

When you multiply a complex number by its conjugate, all the `i`'s go away! And it is formed simply by reversing the sign of the imaginary term. So the complex conjugate of `(3+4i)` is simply `(3-4i)`.

Ok, that's nice, but how does it help us with division?


Use The Conjugate To Make the `i`s Go Away

The trick is to multiply both the numerator and denominator by the conjugate of the denominator:

`(4+3i)/(2-i) xx (2+i)/(2+i)`

This has the same effect as multiplying by `1`, but we're doing so in a way that removes the imaginary term from the denominator leaving only the real term.

`(4+3i) / (2-i)`
`=>` `((4+3i) xx (2+i)) / ((2-i) xx (2+i))` Multiply numerator and denominator by the complex conjugate of the denominator
`=>` `((4+3i) xx (2+i)) / 5` Apply the FOIL rule to the denominator — which reduces it to a real number
`=>` `(8 + 4i + 6i + 3i^2) / 5` Apply the FOIL rule to the numerator
`=>` `(5 + 10i) / 5` Replace all occurrences of `i^2` with `-1` then gather together the real and imaginary terms
`=>` `(5(1 + 2i)) / 5` Remove a factor of `5` from the numerator
`=>` `1 + 2i` The answer
