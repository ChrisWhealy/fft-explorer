Euler's Number

Leonhard Euler (1707 - 1783) was a Swiss mathematician who is generally regarded as one of the most brilliant mathematicians ever to have lived.

Coming from the city of Basel in a German speaking region of Switzerland, his last name is pronounced "oiler", not "yooler".

The number that now bears Leonhard Euler's name was not in fact derived by him; instead, it was derived by another Swiss mathematician, Jacob Bernoulli (1655 - 1705).

Bernoulli was investigating the nature of compound interest and discovered that no matter how many intervals you divide a year into, when calculating compound interest for each of those intervals, the original sum will never grow by more than a factor of `2.718`.

The formula Bernoulli discovered for calculating this limit is `(1 + 1/n)^n` where `n` is the number of intervals at which compound interest is calculated.

So, over the period of a year, if you calculate interest once every month, `n` would be `12`, and the growth factor is `2.613`. Change `n` to 24 and the growth factor rises to `2.664`.

The key point here is to realise that as `n` becomes larger and larger, you are calculating the `n`th exponent of a number that becomes closer and closer to (but never reaches) `1`. This in turn, means that the answer comes closer and closer to (but never exceeds) a maximum value of `2.718`.

To 30 decimal places, this value is `2.718281828459045235360287471352`

This is an example of a converging infinite series.

Infinite Series for `e`

However, it was Leonhard Euler who demonstrated that the same limit could be calculated using a simpler infinite series.

The formula Euler derived is this: `e = sum_(n=0)^oo 1/(n!)` and when he used the number this calculation produces, he assigned it the letter `e`.

If you're not familiar with this notation, here's how to understand it:

  1. The capital Greek letter sigma `sum_` means "summation"; so we immediately know we will have to add up a sequence of numbers.

    But which numbers?

  2. Below the capital sigma, we can see `n=0` and above, we see the symbol for infinity `oo`. This defines the limits of our range: `n` starts at `0` and goes all the way up to infinity…

  3. Next, we see the formula `1/(n!)`
    This formula tells us how to calculate each term in the series: all we have to do is replace `n` with the next value in the sequence.

    So we end up with this: `e =` `1/(0!)` `+` `1/(1!)` `+` `1/(2!)` `+` `1/(3!)` `+` `1/(4!)` `+` `1/(5!)` `+` `...`
    When each of the factorials are calculated, we get `e =` `1` `+` `1` `+` `1/2` `+` `1/6` `+` `1/24` `+` `1/120` `+` `...`

    You might be wondering how the first term evaluates to `1` when `n=0`.
    This is because `0! = 1`.

However, in practical terms, it is completely impossible to add up an infinite series of numbers, so we'll just add up enough terms to give us a sufficiently accurate value to work with. This is called a partial sum.

Infinite Series for `e^x`

More generally, if you wish to calculate `e` raised to some power `x`, then the formula is very similar:

`e^x = sum_(n=0)^oo (x^n)/(n!)`

Which expands out to `e^x = 1 + x + (x^2)/(2!) + (x^3)/(3!) + (x^4)/(4!) + (x^5)/(5!) + ...`
