Maclaurin Series

The formula for calculating `e^x` is an example of a Maclaurin series.

Many other values can be calculated using a Maclaurin series, but the two that are of particular interest to us are the series for calculating sine and cosine.

`sin(θ) = sum_(n=0)^oo (-1^n xx θ^(2n+1))/((2n+1)!)` which expands out to `sin(θ) = θ - (θ^3)/(3!) + (θ^5)/(5!) - (θ^7)/(7!) + ...`
 
`cos(θ) = sum_(n=0)^oo (-1^n xx θ^(2n))/(2n!)` which expands out to `cos(θ) = 1 - (θ^2)/(2!) + (θ^4)/(4!) - (θ^6)/(6!) + ...`

ex Almost Equals sin(θ) + cos(θ)

But hang on a minute… the infinite series for calculating `sin(θ)` and `cos(θ)` have a very similar structure to the formula for `e^x`.

`e^x = 1 + x + (x^2)/(2!) + (x^3)/(3!) + (x^4)/(4!) + (x^5)/(5!) + ...`

The formula for `e^x` is a sequence of terms where the power of each term goes up by one.

The formula for `sin(θ)` is similar to the formula for `e^x`, but contains only the terms raised to odd powers.

The formula for `cos(θ)` contains only the terms raised to even powers.

Let's try writing out each series so that the odd and even terms line up with each other:

`e^x` `=` `1` `+` `x` `+` `x^2/(2!)` `+` `x^3/(3!)` `+` `x^4/(4!)` `+` `x^5/(5!)` `+` `x^6/(6!)` `+` `x^7/(7!)`
`sin(θ)` `=` `θ` `-` `θ^3/(3!)` `+` `x^5/(5!)` `-` `x^7/(7!)`
`cos(θ)` `=` `1` `-` `θ^2/(2!)` `+` `θ^4/(4!)` `-` `θ^6/(6!)`

If we now combine the `sin` and `cos` series, we get something that almost equals the series for `e^x`.

`e^x` `=` `1` `+` `x` `+` `x^2/(2!)` `+` `x^3/(3!)` `+` `x^4/(4!)` `+` `x^5/(5!)` `+` `x^6/(6!)` `+` `x^7/(7!)`
`sin(θ) + cos(θ)` `=` `1` `+` `θ` `-` `θ^2/(2!)` `-` `θ^3/(3!)` `+` `θ^4/(4!)` `+` `x^5/(5!)` `-` `θ^6/(6!)` `-` `x^7/(7!)`

But there's a problem with the signs…

In the `sin` and `cos` series, the signs alternate positive, negative, postive,negative; but in the series for the Euler number, all the signs are positive.

We need a way to change all the negative terms to positive ones.
