Radians: Another Way To Measure Angles

The most widespread unit for measuring rotation is degrees (indicated by a small circle ° written to the top right of a number). A full circle is divided into 360 degrees, so a half turn is 180 degrees, and quarter turn is 90 degrees etc.

The exact reason for why there are 360 degrees in a circle is unclear, but it is most likely related to the fact that there are approximately 360 days in a year, and the Ancient Babylonians used both a 360 day calendar and a base 60 (or sexagesimal) counting system.

Also, 360 is also a very convenient number for calculation because, except for 7 and 11, it is evenly divisible by every number between 2 and 12.

Pi

It has been known since antiquity that the ratio of a circle's circumference to its diameter is a value just over 3 — although arriving at a precise value turned out to be quite tricky.

This ratio is identified by the Greek letter `pi` or Pi and for many practical purposes, is taken to equal `22/7`.

Even though we cannot calculate its exact value, this ratio crops up so frequently (and sometimes unexpectedly), that it becomes very convenient to define rotation in units of `pi`.

Radians

A radian is a unit of rotation and is defined by creating an arc around a circle, such that the length of the arc equals the radius of the circle.

Knowing that the diameter divides into the circumference `pi` times, and that the diameter is twice the length of the radius, we can write `c = pi d = 2 pi r`.

If we create an angle whose arc length equals one radius, then there will be `2pi` such angles in the length of the circumference, that is, in one full rotation.
