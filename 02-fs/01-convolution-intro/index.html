Convolution: The Simplest Case

To Convolve: from the Latin convolvere meaning con = "with" + volvere = "to roll or twist".

Therefore, to "convolve" literally means to "roll or twist together".

In the case of audio processing, convolution is the process of examining a signal in order to discover not only the individual sine waves from which it is composed, but also what contribution each of those sine waves make in terms of phase and amplitude.

This is done by taking the signal and "rolling it together" with a set of test waves (over some range of frequencies) in such a way that the output tells us to what degree the test wave is present in the signal.

Measuring Out-of-Phaseness

Assuming that "out-of-phaseness" is even a word, we will start with the simplest case in which the signal and test wave both have exactly the same frequency (5Hz), amplitude and phase alignment.

Signal + Test Wave

The the graph below, the signal is the blue wave and the phase shift slider controls a red test wave.

As you move the slider left and right, you are shifting the phase of the red test wave relative to the signal. When the slider is at 0°, the two waves are "in phase" (I.E. they line up perfectly), but when the slider is at 180°, the two waves are in anti-phase (I.E. they are perfectly mis-aligned).

Convolution Curve

In order to quantify the degree of misalignment between these two waves, we have to "convolve" the signal with the test wave. What this means is that for each point from 0° to 360° on the test wave, we will take its value and multiply it by the corresponding value on the signal wave.

As you can see, we get another sine wave whose amplitude matches the test wave's amplitude, but whose position swings both up and down, and left and right depending on how far the test wave has been phase shifted.

Integral of the Convolution Curve

The final step is to calculate the area of the green shaded section under the convolution curve. In other words, we need to calculate the integral of the convolution curve between 0° and 360°.
